#### Entry: Product self-assessment Q&A — 2026-05-24

**Jason said (1):**
> "지금 딸깍 프로젝트는 어떤 제품으로 보이니? 이게 어떤 가치가 있따고 보이니"

**Natural English:**
> "How does the Dalkkak project look as a product right now? And what value do you see in it?"

**Notes:**
- "어떤 제품으로 보이니" → "How does it look as a product" / "What kind of product does it look like"
- "있따고" is a casual / typo'd form of "있다고" → "(do you think) it has"
- Native phrasing for the second sentence in a business context: "What's its value, in your view?"

**More polished version (e.g., to an investor or mentor):**
> "How would you characterize the Dalkkak project as a product today, and where do you see its value?"

**Jason said (2):**
> "한글로해봐"

**Natural English:**
> "Try it in Korean." / "Do it in Korean."

**Notes:**
- "한글로 해봐" — casual imperative. "한글" technically refers to the Korean alphabet (the writing system), while "한국어" refers to the Korean language. In everyday speech they're used interchangeably, but for precision: use "한국어" for "the Korean language" and "한글" for "the script."
- A more precise version of this request: "한국어로 해봐" or "한국어로 답해줘" ("Answer in Korean").

**More polished business version:**
> "Could you respond in Korean?" / "Please answer in Korean."

**Coaching note for Jason:**
- In professional / written English, "answer" or "respond" sounds more natural than the direct "do it in [language]" pattern.
- Imperatives like "do it in X" feel casual in English; in business writing prefer "could you respond in X" or "please reply in X."
